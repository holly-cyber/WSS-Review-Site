The head of American collegiate sport has signalled that his organisation's rules on transgender athletes will remain unchanged, even as a watershed federal court decision sends reverberations through women's sport at every level.

Charlie Baker, president of the NCAA — the body that governs college athletics across the United States — told CBS News' *Face the Nation* at the weekend that no policy shift is expected in the wake of the Supreme Court's 6–3 verdict handed down on 30 June. The majority ruling upheld state legislation in West Virginia and Idaho that prohibits trans women and girls from taking part in female sporting categories.

## A Ban Already in Place

According to The Guardian, the NCAA had already moved to restrict transgender participation well before this verdict arrived. In late January 2025, the organisation closed women's sport programmes to any competitor whose sex at birth was male, or who was undergoing testosterone therapy — a move taken in direct response to an executive order issued by Donald Trump shortly after the start of his second term. Men's sport programmes, which Baker described as "the open network," carry no equivalent restrictions.

Baker acknowledged that the policy was driven by a desire for a consistent national standard, framing the NCAA's position as broadly aligned with the federal administration's direction. He drew a clear distinction, however, between what governs collegiate sport at a national level and what plays out jurisdiction by jurisdiction — a nuance that carries real weight given how fragmented American sports governance is in practice.

## What the Supreme Court Decision Really Means

The court's ruling extends well beyond the NCAA's own remit. A majority concluded that excluding trans women and girls from female sporting categories does not breach Title IX, the civil rights law that prohibits sex-based discrimination within educational institutions. The decision overturned lower-court findings that had previously shielded two transgender student athletes — one at university level, one at secondary school — from exclusion in West Virginia and Idaho respectively.

The Guardian reports that legislation mirroring those two states' laws is already on the books in at least 25 other US states. Critically, a great many school and community sporting competitions sit entirely outside the NCAA's authority, meaning the practical consequences of this ruling reach considerably further than elite college athletics.

## A Vast Policy Shift Built on Tiny Numbers

What lends this debate its particularly charged quality is the disparity between the scale of the legal response and the numbers actually involved. The Guardian notes that Baker himself disclosed to Congress in 2024 that, to his knowledge, fewer than ten transgender athletes were competing among the more than half a million students registered in NCAA sport at that point. It is a strikingly small figure on which to anchor such sweeping legal and institutional change.

For active women following this story, the significance cuts in several directions at once. The ruling and the legislative momentum behind it will determine not only who is permitted to compete, but what broader culture comes to surround women's sport in America — and, increasingly, in countries watching closely from elsewhere. Questions of inclusion, fairness and sporting infrastructure seldom resolve neatly, and legal clarity granted by one ruling has a habit of opening fresh litigation somewhere else, as ongoing cases in states including California and Connecticut already illustrate. The landscape remains far from settled, even if the NCAA regards its own position as fixed.
